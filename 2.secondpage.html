FIRST-AID

⚠ Emergency Disclaimer: These guides are of educational purposes only. In life-threatening situations, call 112 immediately. Nothing here replaces proper first aid training or professional medical care.

CPR (Cardiopulmonary Resuscitation) is a life-saving emergency procedure used when a person’s heart stops beating or they are not breathing.
It helps maintain blood flow and oxygen supply to vital organs, especially the brain, until medical help arrives.
CPR involves giving chest compressions at a steady rate and, if trained, rescue breaths.
The standard cycle is 30 compressions followed by 2 breaths.

Choking happens when an object (like food or a small item) blocks the airway, making it hard or impossible to breathe.
A choking person may clutch their throat, cough forcefully, or be unable to speak or breathe.
In severe cases, their lips or face may turn blue due to lack of oxygen.
Immediate first aid is important—encourage coughing if they can, but if they cannot breathe, perform abdominal thrusts (Heimlich maneuver) to clear the blockage.
Quick action can save a life.

Minor burns are injuries that affect only the outer layer of the skin (first-degree burns).
They usually cause redness, mild swelling, and pain, but no deep tissue damage.
Common causes include brief contact with heat, hot liquids, or sunburn.
First aid involves cooling the burn under running water for 10–15 minutes, avoiding ice, and keeping the area clean.
A sterile, non-stick bandage can be applied if needed.
Minor burns typically heal within a few days without scarring if properly cared for.

Severe bleeding is a serious condition where a person loses a large amount of blood quickly due to a deep cut, injury, or accident.
It can be life-threatening if not controlled immediately.
Signs include heavy blood flow, dizziness, weakness, and pale skin.
First aid involves applying direct pressure to the wound using a clean cloth or bandage, keeping the injured area elevated if possible, and seeking emergency medical help quickly.
Prompt action can prevent shock and save a life.

Infant CPR is an emergency life-saving procedure used when a baby (under 1 year old) is not breathing or has no pulse.
It involves gentle chest compressions using two fingers in the center of the chest, at a depth of about 4 cm, combined with rescue breaths to supply oxygen.
The cycle is usually 30 compressions followed by 2 breaths, repeated continuously until the baby starts breathing or medical help arrives.
Care must be taken to apply less force than in adults due to the infant’s delicate body.
